Title: Adaptive Fuel-Optimal Trajectory Planning for Multi-Planetary Spacecraft Missions
Purpose: This research aims to develop a deep reinforcement learning framework using Proximal Policy Optimization to solve continuous trajectory optimization problems for spacecraft navigating complex multi-planetary missions, with a particular emphasis on handling uncertainties in real-time. The central research question is: How can deep reinforcement learning enable autonomous spacecraft to optimize fuel consumption during multi-gravity-assist trajectories while adaptively correcting for observation and control uncertainties in real-time?
I'll be looking for what reinforcement learning architecture can most effectively learn optimal control policies across different mission phases, how Monte Carlo validation can quantify how robust my policies are under various scenarios, and if reinforcement learning policies can achieve fuel efficiency comparable to or better than traditional methods while providing real-time adaptability.
Background: My interest stems from the challenge of space travel in the relationship between mission distance and fuel cost. Launching a kilogram to low Earth orbit costs roughly $7,342, while delivering a kilogram to the Moon's surface costs about $1.2 million, showing how quickly costs can escalate with mission complexity. Fuel optimization is a necessity to enable affordable exploration of the solar system.
I already conducted preliminary research on this project, with hands-on experience with both the Gymnasium library for creating reinforcement learning environments and the Rebound library for N-body gravitational simulations. My initial attempts with custom environments revealed significant computational inefficiency challenges. However, with the Basilisk astrodynamics simulation framework, I'll have software capable of both faster-than-real-time spacecraft simulation and repeatable Monte Carlo options.
My knowledge of astrodynamics and machine learning serves as the theoretical foundation for this. Additionally, my experience with PPO algorithms and my understanding of the challenges in tuning RL hyperparameters for continuous control problems have prepared me for this research.
Prior Research: The application of RL to spacecraft trajectory is a rapidly evolving field that has had significant recent advances. RL can enable new possibilities for closed-loop autonomous GNC systems by addressing wide operational scenarios and exhibiting generalization capabilities that are adaptive in real-time to changing environmental conditions.
Recent research has investigated using deep RL with Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) to solve continuous high-thrust minimum-fuel trajectory optimization problems that have uncertainties in their state and control. It shows PPO algorithms can effectively handle rendezvous problems in uncertain environments, with Monte Carlo simulations validating the performance.
However, gravity-assist trajectory optimization remains extremely complex. They reduce both flight time and propellant consumption, but bring in optimal control problems and nonlinearity. Traditional methods struggle with the combinatorial explosion of possible sequences and timing windows, motivating the need for more adaptive approaches.
Significance: This research addresses the prohibitive fuel costs associated with missions beyond Mars and Venus, which increases exponentially with required delta-v. Developing methods to minimize fuel consumption can enable missions that were otherwise not possible by saving significant amounts of money, especially with agencies planning increasingly ambitious missions to the outer solar system. This research tackles a real-world problem with immediate applicability to ongoing and planned space missions. NASA's planned Mars Sample Return mission will cost between $8 billion and $11 billion, demonstrating the scale of investment that can be optimized with even modest improvements.
I hope to learn whether RL can bridge the gap between the theoretical optimal trajectory and practical real-time implementation. Traditional methods often require extensive compute and struggle with real-time adaptations where a trained RL policy could provide near-optimal guidance in milliseconds, enabling autonomous course-corrections without ground intervention. This is critical for missions to the outer planets, where communication delays can exceed hours.
This research will demonstrate whether modern RL architectures can handle the full complexity of multi-gravity-assist trajectories while maintaining robustness to realistic uncertainties. While existing literature shows success with simplified scenarios, integrating complex maneuvers with uncertainty remains largely unexplored. My unique perspective combines practical implementation experience with a focus on computationally efficient simulations. I can share my findings with both the aerospace and ML research communities, especially with JPL and the ESA which both have active research in this field and practical use for my results.
Description: This research will combine literature research and computational experimentation leading to simulation-based validation. I'll systematically review recent publications on RL for spacecraft control, gravity-assist optimization, and Monte Carlo techniques. The experimental research will focus on implementing and testing RL-based trajectory optimization within a simulation environment. I'll develop a custom Gymnasium-compatible interface to Basilisk's spacecraft dynamics modules, then I'll implement PPO with both standard feedforward networks and more advanced architectures, like LSTM or transformer-based. I'll design reward functions balancing fuel efficiency and trajectory accuracy while conducting extensive hyperparameter tuning to achieve stable training. Finally, the validation phase will use Monte Carlo simulations with many scenarios per mission profile to quantify the robustness and reliability of learned policies.
I'll end up with a paper documenting methodology and results with analysis. I'll have a working implementation of the RL training pipeline with trained policy networks for benchmark mission scenarios and detailed validation results and analysis with visualizations of trajectories demonstrating its performance. All code and models will be documented and made open-source.
Methodology: I'll begin with a systematic literature review through key papers for a comprehensive background section. I'll install and configure the Basilisk simulation framework and understand the architecture. I'll study the API to identify optimal integration points for RL control. I'll design the Markov Decision Process formulation with state space, action space, and preliminary reward structure.
Then, I'll implement a Gymnasium wrapper for Basilisk to initialize the spacecraft, execute thrust commands, observe the spacecraft state, compute rewards, and detect episode termination. I'll create simplified mission scenarios as an initial test case. I'll implement a baseline PPO algorithm and begin initial training runs to identify training instabilities. I'll iterate on the reward function based on the policy's behaviors. Based on initial results, I'll implement more sophisticated policy architectures and expand mission scenarios while modifying hyperparameters.
Finally, I'll develop a Monte Carlo simulation framework with comprehensive validation campaigns. I'll compare the performance against optimal trajectories from traditional models and simple heuristic controllers, comparing fuel efficiency, robustness to uncertainties, and computational cost.
Throughout this project I'll maintain strong documentation and regular check-ins with my advisor to review progress and address challenges.
 Problems: Several significant challenges may arise during this research. Training RL can be extremely computationally intensive, which may require me to simplify scenarios, optimize my integration, reduce initial simulation fidelity, and explore cloud computing. Deep RL is notoriously difficult to tune, and policies may fail to learn effective behaviors, so I may need to start with simplified scenarios while carefully designing the reward and hyperparameters to ensure it is learning something meaningful. Comparing my approach to optimal solutions may be difficult without access to proprietary mission planning tools, so I may only have access to real mission trajectories and may need to emphasize relative comparisons with a heuristic baseline rather than absolute optimality. Finally, the project is ambitious in its scope and may prove too complex for a 3-month timeline. I'll ensure that I have a minimal viable implementation as the core deliverables with multi-gravity-assist trajectories as stretch goals.
